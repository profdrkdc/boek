
> _The only reason we live is to ensure that the people who come after us have a better life than we do._


# Chapter 1: The world we live in is not perfect, not finished, not complete, not done...

There is much suffering and injustice in the world. But the universe is not against us — it is neither good nor evil. It is a mechanism that operates according to immutable laws. It simply is what it is, without passing moral judgment on us. The universe is the raw structure, the rough foundation we have been given, and where nature ceases to be in our favour, our task begins: to build a better world. Our fundamental problem is not the good or evil in the world, but the unfinishedness and imperfection of the world. That is the core of our diagnosis: nature is merely the foundation, and that is what we must work with. The world is like an unfinished construction site — and there is still a great deal of work to be done.

The only thing that can save us from ourselves and from earthly suffering is the responsibility we bear toward the children of tomorrow, toward the people of the future, toward the generations that come after us. Our responsibility toward the children of tomorrow and toward the generations of the future is our only inescapable, unsurpassable source of meaning — the only thing that can offer comfort in the face of all the senseless suffering and injustice we encounter as human beings. It is the only thing that creates value and generates meaning in life.

We are not a sinful species. We are a species that has much to learn. We, and our ancestors, were not given a manual for life. Through living we gained experience. And from experience we learn, we gather knowledge. Knowledge we pass on each time. Knowledge that enables us to understand the world — including ourselves — a little better, and to understand how we can change and improve it. Learning is not always straightforward; it is a process that requires time and effort, and sometimes that time is not granted to us. But today, more than ever, it is becoming clear that we can take our responsibility in this... can and must take it. We can, and must, take matters into our own hands. We are the ones who can give direction to the future. That responsibility has been given to us.

By learning from mistakes of the past — mistakes that were usually made out of ignorance or fear — we must give direction to the future. Out of responsibility toward those who come after us, who are in fact largely dependent on us for their wellbeing and happiness, for the kind of world they are given. Our history was, and is, full of struggle, misery, and hardship, and the undeniable line of progress was not always straight, not always swift — but over the long term, progress has become visible.

It places us in the clear light of reality: the world, life, is no picnic — but we can build the capacities to do something about it. It is a task before us, and we have tools in our hands. Human beings have the unique gift of adding compassion and care to a world that does not possess these by default. The people of the future are entirely dependent on us, and in essence they are no different from us. They will have the same needs, the same desires, the same fears, the same dreams. They may even look a little like us. The only difference is that they will live in a world we have created — a world that will be better or worse depending on the choices we make today.

Disease is a biochemical puzzle we can solve. An earthquake is a geological fact we can prepare for. That gives us a responsibility we cannot simply ignore.

This book is not a search for the 'why' of the past. That question leads us astray. We look toward the 'how' of the future. This book is an invitation to intellectual emancipation on the scale of civilisation as a whole — an invitation to act. The world is a system with bugs, and we are the debuggers, the programmers. The architects and the builders. Suffering is the product of an outdated system; wellbeing must be the product of the new system.

This book is an invitation — not to *endure* the world, but a strategy to improve it, stone by stone, system by system, insight by insight. This evolution does not begin with blind faith. It begins with a sharp, clear vision of what our possibilities and our limitations are.

Welcome to the workshop. The real work can begin.

Let us inspect the world. Not as critics who complain about the cracks in the wall, but as engineers who come to reinforce its inner structure. Human suffering is no mystery — it is a logical and sometimes material, logistical problem that calls for a solution. And we have the capacity to start the two engines of progress: mastery of nature and cultivation of the human spirit.

Let us begin with nature. We romanticise nature far too often and far too easily. Let us not look away from its flaws, but let us also set to work repairing them. A virus is simply code we can rewrite or delete; cancer is a biological error that calls for correction. We have the science to patch the operating system of life, and our medical technology draws closer to that solution every day. Let us be a healing force — and not the death blow to nature and to life on this planet.

Nature chooses brutal efficiency and survival; we choose the efficiency that transcends this — the efficiency of responsibility. We can develop agriculture and technology that nourishes without destroying. We need not imitate the cruelty of the wild; we can design an ecosystem that runs on sustainability and care. A design to be proud of.

The greatest opportunity for improvement lies within ourselves. We are the nature that has gained consciousness. Our intellect enables us not only to see errors, but to resolve them structurally. Our history is a learning process. We possess so much potential, yet there is still so much work that awaits us. War is proof that we must design better mechanisms for conflict management — and we are doing so. Diplomacy, psychology, and international cooperation are technologies we continue to refine. It is the triumph of reason over instinct.

Improvement also lies in our everyday systems. Politics need not be a contest of the loudest voices; it is a platform for competent problem-solvers. We can choose leaders and build systems oriented toward solutions rather than conflict.

In the end, those who build solutions will always be the ones who remain, who are more successful, who are the future. Those who do not learn, who do not evolve, who do not continue to develop, will lose — will lose themselves, will have no future.

The justice system is in need of revision. We can replace bureaucracy with clarity and efficiency. We can strive for a system that genuinely serves justice, designed to find the truth and heal society.

Finally, the most intimate form of progress is shaped within the family. The father who transforms his powerlessness into wisdom and patience. He plants the seed of self-confidence and resilience. The mother who learns and grows, and teaches her children that love and safety go hand in hand. This is not 'happiness'. This is competence in love. It is the ultimate cultivation of human relationships.

From the microscopic battle against disease to our daily interactions with those we love: the pattern is the same. It is the optimisation of a system. It is the noble task not merely to protect life, but to let it flourish. The happiness that flows from this is real and earned. But above all it is the happiness of succession that counts — only when we live for the people of the future, for the generations to come, can we be truly happy. For the happiness we give them, that they share with us, is far greater than any happiness we can bestow upon ourselves.
This is the reality we may embrace. Not a tragedy, but a future — a future being built, here and now, by us.

~

The facts are on the table. From the blind randomness of a virus to the destructive logic of war; from the indifference of natural laws to the inability of a parent to process their own trauma and therefore not pass it on to their children. The pattern is clear and the conclusion is sober.

The world is a system with fundamental flaws.

This is not pessimism, but a technical observation. The system falters. Its output contains, alongside beauty and life, an unacceptable amount of friction, pain, and destruction. For centuries we have tried to explain this suffering with myths. We spoke of fate, divine trials, or karma. We built elaborate theological and philosophical structures — not so much to solve the problem, but to make suffering bearable. They were necessary supporting pillars to preserve our mental health at a time when we were powerless.

But these old frameworks of thought have reached their expiry date. They fall short because they are often based on powerlessness as their starting point. However human these solutions may be, they are essentially a dead end. The suggestion that somewhere there lies a hidden script, a moral reason we are overlooking, an authority that governs our lives...
If we look honestly at reality — at the cosmos and evolution — there are no hidden scripts. The idea of higher power(s), of predestination, of a fixed script leads to passive resignation ('It must be so'), to paralysing despair ('There is no point'), or to dogmatic intransigence. These reactions solve nothing.

A clear mind asks a different question. The question of the builder, the architect, the physician, the caregiver. Not 'Why is the machine broken?', but '*How* does the defect work and how do we repair it?' Or, more directly:

**"What do we do about it?"**

This is the question that makes the difference. It distinguishes passive suffering from active intervention. An animal endures pain; the human being has the unique capacity to analyse the cause of the pain and eliminate it. That capacity is our greatest strength in a universe that possesses no morality of its own. Not using that insight is a waste of our potential.

This book is built around that question. It is an attempt at a workable answer. Expect no false promises or soft comfort that denies reality. The world is hard and change is difficult. What follows is not a philosophy to drift away in, but a strategy to get to work with. It is a sober search for the competence needed to improve our circumstances, step by step.


We have laid bare the foundation of the problem. The diagnosis is not that the world is a 'hell', but that it is *unfinished*. It is governed by natural laws that are blind to our happiness, and inhabited by people who are still learning to deal with their own potential and their own shortcomings. We have established that suffering is a real problem that calls for practical solutions, not mystical excuses.

The conclusion of this analysis is as simple as it is demanding. The responsibility lies with us. If there is no ready-made plan, we must design one. If the system contains errors, it is our task to develop the skills to correct those errors.

The first step has been taken: the honest acknowledgement of the state we are in, without embellishment. Now the next phase begins: critically examining the systems that are not working and identifying the obstacles — in the world and within ourselves — that stand in the way of progress. Reality has been mapped. Let us now look at what is needed to rebuild it. The world is imperfect. There is no external 'plan', and the responsibility to improve the system lies entirely with us.

***

The solution lies in **constructive realism**: acknowledging the hard facts (suffering, the absence of an external plan, human fallibility) without falling into cynicism ("everything is hell") or naivety ("everything will work out"). The problem is serious, but solvable — if we ask the right questions.
