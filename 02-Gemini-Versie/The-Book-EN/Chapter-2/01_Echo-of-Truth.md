# Chapter 2: The Constitution of Existence

Let us begin with the only truth that matters. It is not a truth revealed on a mountaintop or in a holy book, but a law inscribed in the structure of reality itself. It is the constitution of existence, and it reads as follows:

*No species, no group, no civilisation can survive in the long run if it does not regard the interests and wellbeing of future generations as its highest purpose.*

This is not a moral assumption. It is not a pious wish. It is the iron, inescapable logic of evolution, applied to everything that exists. A system that undermines its own future selects itself for extinction. It is the ultimate test, the only test that counts. A civilisation that exhausts its resources, poisons its environment, and offers its children no prospects will disappear. An idea that creates no value for the next generation dies out. A way of life focused solely on immediate gratification consumes itself into oblivion.

This one, fundamental law is the source of all ethics.

Ethics is not a human invention. It is not a set of arbitrary rules we devised to make living together more pleasant. Ethics is the survival protocol of a conscious species. 'Good' is that which ensures the continued existence and wellbeing of future generations. 'Evil' is that which endangers that future. Murder is not 'evil' because a god forbids it; it is 'evil' because it destroys the most elementary building block of the future — a life. Deception is not 'evil' because it is a sin; it is 'evil' because it destroys the trust that is essential for the long-term cooperation needed to solve complex problems.

From this perspective, we must look at the great spiritual and religious traditions of the world with fresh eyes. They are not collections of outdated myths, but humanity's first brilliant attempts to discover and codify this one deep truth. Our ancestors did not have the language of genetics or systems theory, so they used the language they had: that of metaphor, story, and sacred commandment.

The concept of **'Karma'** is nothing more than a poetic expression of this law. The idea that our actions inevitably have consequences that return to us is the recognition that we are part of an interconnected system that reaches across time. Actions that strengthen the system (good karma) benefit us — not through a mystical force, but through the logical consequences of a healthier, more stable system for our descendants.

The idea of **'life as a test'** is the literal recognition of this evolutionary reality. Every day, every decision is a test: does this contribute to continuity, or does it accelerate the decline? Our ancestors understood that we are constantly being weighed and measured — not by a judging deity, but by inescapable reality itself.

Even the idea of **'eternal life'** or **'reincarnation'** can be seen as an intuitive grasp at the truth of genetic and cultural inheritance. It is the recognition that something of us — our information, our values, the consequences of our deeds — lives on in the generations that follow. Our immortality lies not in heaven, but in the future we help to shape.

The great stories of humanity are therefore not lies. They are the first, indispensable chapters in our handbook for survival. They represent the discovery, through trial and error, of the most crucial strategy of all: focus on the future, or you have none.

Science has since given us a sharper language to describe this principle. We can now lay bare the *mechanism* behind the metaphor. But the fundamental message — the echo of the truth that resonates throughout all ancient wisdom — has remained the same.

Our task now is to embrace this truth, not as a vague spiritual notion, but as the hard, concrete, and central organising principle of our civilisation. The question is no longer whether we believe in God, Karma, or evolution. The question is whether we recognise the constitution of existence and have the courage to live by it. For the test is now under way, and the future is watching.
