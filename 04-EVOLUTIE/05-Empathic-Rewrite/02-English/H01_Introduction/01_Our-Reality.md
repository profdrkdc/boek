# Our Reality

> _The only reason we live is to ensure that those who come after us inherit a better world than the one we were given._

The only thing that can save us from ourselves and from earthly suffering is the responsibility we bear toward the children of tomorrow, toward the people of the future, toward the generations that follow us. Our responsibility is our only inescapable, insurmountable source of meaning—the only thing that can offer solace in the face of all the senseless suffering and injustice we confront as human beings. It is the only thing that creates value and generates meaning in life.

We are a species with much to learn. We, and our ancestors, were not given a manual for life. Through the act of living, we gained experience. And from experience, we learn; we accumulate knowledge. Knowledge that we pass on, time and again. Knowledge that enables us to understand the world bit by bit and to grasp how we can change and improve both the world and ourselves. Learning is not always easy; it is a process that demands time and effort, and sometimes we are not granted enough of either. But today, more than ever, it is evident that we can take our responsibility in this... we can and we must. We can, and must, take control. WE are the ones who can give direction to the future. That responsibility has been bestowed upon us.

By learning from the mistakes of the past—mistakes mostly born of ignorance or fear—we must chart the course of the future. Out of responsibility toward those who follow us, who are de facto largely dependent on us for their well-being and happiness, and for the kind of world they are given. Our history was, and is, full of struggle and misery. The undeniable line of progress was not always linear, nor always swift, but over the long term, that progress has become visible.

It places us in the clear light of reality: the world, life itself, is no picnic. But we can build the capacity to do something about it. It is a task we face, and we hold the tools in our hands. Humans have the unique gift of adding compassion and care to a world that does not naturally possess them. The people of the future are entirely dependent on us, and in essence, they are no different from us. They will have the same needs, the same desires, the same fears, the same dreams. They might even look a bit like us. The only difference is that they will live in a world we created—a world that will be better or worse depending on the choices we make today.

There is much suffering and injustice in the world. But the universe is not against us; it is neither good nor evil. It is a mechanism operating according to immutable laws. It simply is what it is, without passing moral judgment upon us. The universe is the framework, the raw foundation given to us, and where nature ceases to be favorable to us, there begins our task to build a better world. Our fundamental problem is not the presence of good or evil in the world, but the incompleteness and imperfection of the world. That is the core of our diagnosis: nature is merely the foundation, and we must get to work with it. The world is like an unfinished construction site—and there is still much work to be done.

### The Law of Convergence: Why We Must Evolve

To understand *why* we must perform this work, we must look at the two forces that define our reality.

First, there is the **individual starting point**: the inner demand that everyone seeks to be happy. This is our fundamental drive, the engine behind every human action.

Second, there is the **extra-individual starting point**: the external demand of the environment. In reality, only those survive who possess fitting characteristics that make survival possible in a given circumstance. But possessing these traits is not enough; they must also be used in the correct manner.

This leads us to a crucial conclusion. The greatest chance of survival, success, and a lasting place in the future lies with those who are so formed—through predisposition or conscious development—that they possess fitting traits AND that it makes them happy to use these traits correctly.

When our inner drive for happiness coincides (converges) with what is externally required for survival, the most powerful form of existence emerges.

These traits are not static. They can be part of our **Hardware** (biological predisposition), our **Software** (what we learn and program into our behavior), or even our **Extended Hardware** (technological extensions such as prosthetics or brain-computer interfaces).

Those who naturally—or through conscious effort—evolve toward fitting, functional traits that effectively make life easier and themselves happier, are the ones who will shape the future. They are the architects of the system.

Disease is a biochemical puzzle we can solve. An earthquake is a geological fact we can arm ourselves against. This gives us a responsibility we cannot simply ignore.

This book is not a search for the 'why' of the past. That question distracts us. We look toward the 'how' of the future. This book is an invitation to intellectual emancipation on the scale of entire civilizations; it is an invitation to entrepreneurship. The world is a system with bugs, and we are the debuggers, the programmers. The architects and the builders. Suffering is the product of an outdated system; well-well-being must be the product of the new system.

This book is an invitation, not to *endure* the world, but a strategy to improve it—stone by stone, system by system, insight by insight. This evolution does not begin with blind faith. It begins with a sharp, clear vision of what our possibilities and limitations are.

Welcome to the workshop. The real work can begin.

Let us inspect the world. Not as critics complaining about the cracks in the wall, but as engineers arriving to strengthen the inner structure. Human suffering is not a mystery; it is a logical and sometimes material, logistical problem that demands a solution. And we have the capacity to start the two engines of progress: the mastery of nature and the cultivation of the human spirit.

Let us begin with nature. We romanticize nature far too often and far too easily. Let us not look away from its flaws, but let us also set to work repairing them. A virus is simply code we can rewrite or delete; cancer is a biological error that demands correction. We have the science to patch the operating system of life, and our medical technology draws closer to that solution every day. Let us be a healing force, not the death blow to nature and to life on this planet.

Nature chooses brute efficiency and survival; let us choose the transcending efficiency of responsibility. We can develop agriculture and technology that nourish without destroying. We do not need to imitate the cruelty of the wild; we can design an ecosystem that runs on sustainability and care. A design to be proud of.

The greatest opportunity for improvement lies within ourselves. Our intellect enables us not only to see mistakes but to solve them structurally. Our history is a learning process. We possess so much potential, yet so much work still awaits us. War is the proof that we must design better mechanisms for conflict management—and we are doing so. Diplomacy, psychology, and international cooperation are technologies we continue to refine. It is the triumph of reason over instinct.

Improvement also lies within our daily systems. Politics need not be a battle of the loudest shouters; it is a platform for competent problem solvers. We can choose leaders and build systems aimed at solutions rather than conflicts.

Ultimately, those who build toward solutions will always be the ones who remain, who are more successful, who ARE the future. Those who do not learn, who do not evolve, who do not further develop themselves, will lose, will lose themselves, and will not belong to the future.

The legal system is overdue for revision. We can replace bureaucracy with clarity and efficiency. We can strive for a system that truly serves justice, designed to find the truth and to heal society.

Finally, the most intimate form of progress is shaped within the family. The father who transforms his powerlessness into wisdom and patience. He plants the seeds of self-confidence and resilience. The mother who learns and grows, teaching her children that love and safety go hand in hand. This is competence in love, the ultimate cultivation of human relationships.

From the microscopic battle against disease to the daily interaction with our loved ones: the pattern is the same. it is the optimization of a system. It is the noble task not just to protect life, but to let it flourish. The happiness that results from this is real and earned. But it is primarily the happiness of succession that counts. Only if we live for the people of the future, the generations to come, can we truly be happy. For the happiness we give them, that they share with us, is far greater than any happiness we can bestow upon ourselves.

This is the reality we are allowed to embrace. Not a tragedy, but a future—a future being built, here and now, by us.

The facts are on the table. From the blind randomness of a virus to the destructive logic of a war; from the indifference of natural laws to the inability of a parent to process their own trauma rather than passing it on to their children. The pattern is clear and the conclusion is sober.

The world is a system with fundamental flaws.

This is not pessimism, but a technical observation. The system is faltering. Its output contains, alongside beauty and life, an unacceptable amount of friction, pain, and destruction. For centuries, we have tried to explain this suffering with myths. We spoke of fate, divine trials, or karma. We built elaborate theological and philosophical structures, not so much to solve the problem, but to make the suffering endurable. These were necessary buttresses to preserve our mental health in a time when we were powerless.

But these old conceptual frameworks have reached their expiration date. They fall short because they are often based on powerlessness as a starting point. However human these solutions may be, in essence, they are a dead end. The suggestion that there lies a hidden script somewhere, a moral reason we are overlooking, an authority that determines our lives...

If we look honestly at reality—at the cosmos and evolution—there are no hidden scripts. The idea of higher power(s), of predestination, of a fixed script leads to passive resignation ("It must be so"), to paralyzing despair ("It’s pointless"), or to dogmatic intransigence. These reactions solve nothing.

A clear mind asks a different question. The question of the builder, the architect, the doctor, the caregiver. Not "Why is the machine broken?" but "*How* does the defect work and how do we repair it?" Or, even more directly:

**"What do we do about it?"**

This is the question that makes the difference. It distinguishes passive endurance from active intervention. An animal endures pain; humans have the unique capacity to analyze the cause of pain and eliminate it. That capacity is our greatest strength in a universe that possesses no inherent morality. Failing to use that insight is a waste of our potential.

This book is built around that question. It is an attempt at a workable answer. Expect no false promises or soft comfort that denies reality. The world is harsh and change is difficult. What follows is not a philosophy for dreaming, but a strategy for action. It is a sober search for the competence required to improve our circumstances, step by step.

We have exposed the root of the problem. The diagnosis is not that the world is a 'hell', but that it is *unfinished*. It is governed by laws of nature that are blind to our happiness, and inhabited by people still learning to manage their own potential and their own flaws. We have established that suffering is a real problem demanding practical solutions, not mystical excuses.

The conclusion of this analysis is as simple as it is demanding. The responsibility lies with us. If there is no ready-made plan, we must design one. If the system contains errors, it is our task to develop the skills to fix those errors.

Life has no meaning unless we give it meaning ourselves. It is a blank canvas, and we are the painters. We determine what goes on the canvas. We choose the colors, the shapes, the composition. We decide whether it becomes a masterpiece or a chaos. It is a massive responsibility, but it is also an incredible opportunity. The opportunity to create something larger than ourselves, something that will stand the test of time, something that will change the world.

The first step has been taken: the honest recognition of the state we are in, without embellishment. The world is not fair unless we make it fair. Now begins the next phase: the critical examination of the systems that do not work and the identification of the obstacles—in the world and in ourselves—that stand in the way of progress. Reality has been mapped. Let us now look at what is needed to improve it. The world is imperfect. There is no external 'plan' and the responsibility to improve the system lies with us.

***

The solution lies in **constructive realism**. Recognizing the hard facts (suffering, lack of an external plan, human fallibility) without falling into cynicism ("everything is hell") or naivety ("everything will be fine"). The problem is serious, but solvable if we ask the right questions.
